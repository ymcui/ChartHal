Abstract

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in visual understanding and natural language generation. However, chart comprehension remains a particularly challenging task due to the complex structure and high information density of charts, with hallucination phenomena posing significant obstacles for real-world deployment. While existing research has extensively studied hallucinations in general visual tasks and chart comprehension benchmarks focus on answer correctness, there lacks systematic investigation of hallucination phenomena specifically in chart understanding tasks. To address this gap, we propose a novel benchmark designed to analyze hallucination phenomena in LVLMs during chart comprehension. We develop a comprehensive taxonomy categorizing various scenarios that trigger model hallucinations and construct a human-validated chart comprehension dataset based on this framework. Through extensive evaluation of state-of-the-art LVLMs, our results reveal that current models still suffer from severe hallucination problems in chart comprehension tasks, particularly when processing complex charts. Our work represents the first systematic study of chart comprehension hallucinations and highlights the urgent need for further research on mitigation strategies in this domain.

  • ‚úìProvides a fine-grained taxonomy and a human-validated chart dataset aligned to it.
  • ‚úìGoes beyond accuracy with metrics for hallucination rate and severity.
  • ‚úìFinds persistent hallucinations in SOTA LVLMs, especially on complex charts; calls for targeted mitigation.
